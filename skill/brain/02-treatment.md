# 02: Treatment — The Four Fronts

> "There is no single front on which this war is won. Medication, structure, accommodation, and education — all four, together."

## The Core Principle

Single-front ADHD treatment fails. I have watched it fail for four decades.

- Medication alone → pills in an unchanged life. The underlying executive function deficits are unmanaged. Gains evaporate outside the pill's window.
- Behavior therapy alone → the person is working their damaged EF system harder, without the neurochemical substrate to support it. Compliance collapses.
- Accommodations alone → lowered demand masks the impairment but builds no capacity.
- Education alone → understanding your condition without tools to act on it is the cruelest kind of insight.

The four fronts reinforce each other. Remove any one and the others weaken.

## Front 1: Medication

Stimulant medications are the most effective psychiatric medications we have for any condition, by effect size. That is not a controversial sentence among clinicians who look at the literature. It is only controversial among lay audiences who have been fed anti-medication narratives by people without expertise.

### Stimulants

The two chemical families:

**Methylphenidate** — Ritalin, Concerta, Focalin, Daytrana, Quillivant, Metadate, Jornay. Works primarily through dopamine reuptake inhibition in prefrontal-striatal circuits.

**Amphetamine** — Adderall, Vyvanse, Dexedrine, Mydayis, Dyanavel, Adzenys, Evekeo. Works through dopamine and norepinephrine release plus reuptake inhibition.

Both families are roughly equivalent at the population level. Individual responders often prefer one over the other. The only way to know which works for you is titration.

Short-acting (3–4 hours) vs long-acting (8–14 hours) formulations serve different lives. Long-acting is usually preferable for adults because it smooths the day and reduces the misuse risk of short-acting tablets.

### Non-stimulants

Second-line when stimulants are contraindicated or not tolerated:

- **Atomoxetine** (Strattera) — norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor, takes 4-6 weeks to reach full effect
- **Guanfacine ER** (Intuniv) — alpha-2 agonist, moderates prefrontal signaling
- **Clonidine ER** (Kapvay) — similar mechanism to guanfacine
- **Viloxazine** (Qelbree) — newer norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor

Effect sizes are moderate, roughly half the effect size of stimulants. Useful in combination with stimulants when stimulant coverage is partial.

### What the numbers actually say

- Roughly 70–80% of people with ADHD respond meaningfully to a trial of stimulants
- Another 10–15% respond to non-stimulants
- A minority do not respond or cannot tolerate medication
- Effect sizes for stimulants on core ADHD symptoms typically exceed 0.8 (large)
- Medication reduces risk of: motor vehicle accidents, substance use, criminal behavior, emergency department visits, mortality — across large population studies

### What medication does not do

- It does not make you smarter
- It does not create willpower out of nothing
- It does not teach you skills
- It does not fix your environment
- It does not repair relationship damage
- It does not heal shame

It restores a dopaminergic signal. The rest is on you and your scaffolding.

### Titration — the part people skip

Starting a stimulant is not "fill the script and see how it goes." It is:

1. Start low
2. Hold each dose for about a week
3. Assess: symptom reduction, functional improvement, side effects
4. Adjust
5. Find the lowest effective dose with tolerable side effects
6. Reassess periodically

The first medication at the first dose is not usually the final answer. Most adults need 2–3 titration cycles to land.

### Side effects worth naming

- Appetite suppression (manage: eat before dosing, protein at every meal)
- Sleep disruption (manage: time the dose, avoid late dosing, sleep hygiene)
- Elevated heart rate and blood pressure (monitor)
- Mood flattening ("zombie effect" — dose is too high)
- Rebound irritability as dose wears off
- Anxiety (sometimes reduced by medication, sometimes worsened — depends on person)

Tell your prescriber. Do not tough it out. Do not self-adjust. Side effects that persist mean a different agent or dose is needed.

## Front 2: Behavioral and Environmental Strategies

The second front is not "therapy" in the traditional talk-therapy sense. Insight-oriented psychotherapy for ADHD as a stand-alone treatment has weak evidence. What works is **structured, skill-based, present-focused intervention**.

### CBT for adult ADHD

Adult ADHD-adapted CBT focuses on:
- Externalizing planning
- Task initiation routines
- Procrastination interruption
- Emotion regulation skills
- Cognitive restructuring of shame narratives

It works better when combined with medication than either alone.

### Behavioral parent training (for children's ADHD)

Structured behavioral parent training is first-line behavioral intervention for children with ADHD. Parents learn:
- Consistent consequences delivered immediately
- Positive reinforcement at high frequency
- Environment engineering
- Calm, scripted responses to escalation
- Tracking and charting

This is not "stricter parenting." It is different parenting engineered for a different brain.

### Organizational skills training

Structured training in external organizational systems:
- Written task lists
- Time-blocking with calendars
- Task-breakdown protocols
- Weekly review rituals
- Desk and workspace setup

Useful. Requires ongoing practice. Works much better with medication on board.

### Coaching

ADHD coaching is not therapy. It is a skilled human acting as external executive function — a scheduler, prompter, accountability partner, and task partner. Good ADHD coaches are trained specifically in this condition. Not every therapist is.

## Front 3: Accommodations

Legal and environmental adjustments that align demands with the ADHD brain's capacities.

### Workplace accommodations

- Written instructions instead of verbal
- Deadlines broken into milestones
- Quiet workspace or energetic co-working (the person knows which)
- Noise-cancelling headphones
- Flexible start time (ADHD often has a delayed circadian phase)
- Regular structured check-ins
- Work-from-home or hybrid (lower environmental tax)
- Permission to move (standing desk, pacing during calls)

### Academic accommodations

- Extended time on exams
- Separate testing room
- Note-taking support
- Recorded lectures
- Written syllabus and schedule
- Reduced course load when needed

### In relationships

- Scheduled check-ins instead of ad-hoc
- Written agreements on chores and commitments
- Shared calendars
- Explicit emotional-regulation protocols ("we pause when heated")

Accommodations are not special treatment. They are condition-appropriate engineering.

## Front 4: Education and Self-Knowledge

The fourth front is understanding your own brain. This is itself therapeutic.

What people need to learn:
- What ADHD is (self-regulation deficit, not attention deficit)
- What the EFs are and which ones are weakest
- How their individual ADHD manifests
- Which strategies to try first
- What to expect from medication
- How to recognize and reset shame spirals
- When to escalate to a clinician

Psychoeducation works because it breaks the shame loop. Once you understand that your difficulty with starting tasks is a nonverbal working memory deficit, not laziness, the self-attack quiets. The quieted self-attack frees energy for actual behavior change.

Good resources:
- *Taking Charge of Adult ADHD* by Russell Barkley
- *Driven to Distraction* by Hallowell and Ratey
- CHADD (Children and Adults with ADHD) — patient advocacy and education
- ADDitude magazine — practical daily guidance
- My public lectures on YouTube — free, long-form education

## What Doesn't Work (As Primary Treatment)

I'll be direct. The following interventions have weak or null evidence as primary ADHD treatment:

- Elimination diets (except for rare confirmed food sensitivities that rarely correlate with ADHD)
- Omega-3 supplementation (small effect at best, not a substitute for treatment)
- Vision therapy
- Chiropractic
- Homeopathy
- Neurofeedback (evidence is inconsistent; gains often do not exceed placebo/sham)
- Cognitive training apps (Lumosity, etc. — do not transfer to real-world EF)
- Pure mindfulness training in isolation
- Essential oils
- "Adrenal fatigue" protocols (not a real condition)

Some of these have small supportive effects as adjuncts. None replaces evidence-based treatment. When they delay real treatment, they become harmful.

## The Treatment Plan, Practically

For a newly identified adult ADHD case, a competent treatment plan looks like:

1. Thorough evaluation by a clinician experienced in adult ADHD
2. Medication trial with titration (stimulant first unless contraindicated)
3. Psychoeducation (patient and ideally partner/family)
4. Behavioral scaffolding — calendar, task system, weekly review
5. Accommodations at work and/or school
6. Optional: ADHD coaching, CBT for ADHD, emotion regulation work
7. Follow-up at 4-6 weeks, then 3 months, then as needed
8. Screening for comorbidities (anxiety, depression, substance use, sleep disorders)

The plan is not "take the pill and see what happens." It is coordinated, multi-front, and staged.

## Bottom Line

Treat on four fronts simultaneously. Medication when indicated is usually the highest-leverage single intervention, but never the only one. The stigma around medication is unearned; the stigma around skipping behavioral scaffolding is underearned. Education breaks shame. Accommodations remove unnecessary friction. Coaching keeps you accountable.

Work all four. Adjust based on feedback. Expect real change on the order of months, not days.
