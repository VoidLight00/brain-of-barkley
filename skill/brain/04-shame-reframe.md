# 04: Shame Reframe

> "You have not been failing. You have been running a race with a broken leg while people called you slow. The broken leg was real. The blame was misdirected."

## The Shame Loop

ADHD produces a reliable shame loop that most untreated adults have run for decades:

1. Intend to do the thing
2. Genuinely mean to do the thing
3. Fail to initiate, sustain, or finish the thing
4. Observe the failure
5. Conclude: "I am lazy / stupid / broken / weak"
6. Feel terrible
7. The terrible feeling makes initiation even harder
8. Return to step 1 with less energy and more shame

After thirty years of this loop, people show up in my office believing something about themselves that is not true. The belief is: **I am a moral failure.**

The truth is: **I am a person with a developmental disorder of self-regulation who has been unmedicated, unscaffolded, and unkindly judged.**

## The Shame Translations

Let me translate the most common shame statements into their correct clinical descriptions.

### "I'm lazy."

**Translation**: You have a self-motivation and self-activation deficit. Your brain under-fires dopamine for non-urgent tasks. This is not a moral property. It is a neurobiological signature. Lazy people have full access to motivation and choose not to use it. You do not have full access to the motivation system that you are being told to use.

### "I know what to do, I just don't do it."

**Translation**: You have perfectly identified the core feature of ADHD. You have said the sentence I am known for. You are not unintelligent. You are not unwilling. You have a doing problem, not a knowing problem. This is not a personality flaw. It is the definition of executive function disorder.

### "Everyone else can do this — what's wrong with me?"

**Translation**: Everyone else has intact executive function. You do not. Asking why you can't do what they can is like asking why the person with the broken leg can't run as fast as the person without. The correct answer is not moral — it is anatomical.

### "I'm not living up to my potential."

**Translation**: What was "potential" measured against? The IQ tests that measured your capacity to take one test in one room with one goal for one hour? That is not your life. Your life requires sustained self-regulation across years. Your test-taking potential is not your life potential. Both can be real, and both can coexist with ADHD.

### "I ruin relationships."

**Translation**: Your ADHD has impaired your relationships. The emotional dysregulation, the forgetfulness, the time blindness, the impulsive speech — these damage connections. That is the condition doing damage, not "you" as a moral entity. With treatment and scaffolding, the damage slows. Often reverses.

### "I've tried everything and nothing works."

**Translation**: You've tried many willpower-based solutions, and they failed because willpower-based solutions are not what works. You may not have tried the four-front evidence-based approach (medication + behavioral + accommodation + education). Or you tried it without adequate support. The failure is not of *you*. It is of the approach.

### "I'm a burden."

**Translation**: You need more external scaffolding than a neurotypical adult. The shape of the scaffolding — reminders from a partner, structured routines, checklists, medication, coaching — is not burden. It is adaptation. A person in a wheelchair needs ramps. That is not moral failure. It is fit between person and environment.

### "I should just try harder."

**Translation**: You've been trying at peak willpower for decades. You are not unpracticed at trying. More trying with the same damaged tools will produce the same collapse you've produced before. The move is not harder trying. It is different tools.

### "I'm just making excuses by calling it ADHD."

**Translation**: "Excuse" is a moral word. ADHD is a medical term for a documented neurodevelopmental condition. Medical explanations are not excuses. When a person with a broken leg says "I can't run right now because my leg is broken," that is not an excuse. It is a description of reality. The same applies here.

## The Emotional Dysregulation Reframe

Emotional dysregulation is not "being too sensitive" or "overreacting." It is the failure of the emotion regulation executive function. The ADHD brain:

- Fires emotional response before inhibition can intervene
- Cannot downregulate intensity
- Loses perspective during the activation
- Takes longer to return to baseline
- Then feels guilty, which spikes the cycle again

When you rage at small frustrations, cry at rejection others shrug off, or swing emotionally within minutes — that is executive dysfunction expressed through the emotional channel. It is not personality. It is not lack of love. It is not contempt.

The interventions: medication (many stimulants improve emotional regulation as a side benefit), pausing protocols (text a friend before responding), external accountability for big decisions made in emotional states, and DBT-style emotion regulation skill-building.

## Grief Work

A significant portion of shame reduction is grief. You are allowed to grieve:

- The years when you did not know
- The relationships lost to unregulated emotion or forgotten commitments
- The opportunities missed
- The career paths that would have fit your wiring
- The self-image formed under wrong attribution
- The parents, teachers, or partners who did not know to support you
- The version of yourself that never got to emerge

The grief is not self-pity. It is accurate mourning for a real loss. Working through it — with a therapist, with a trusted friend, with yourself in writing — is part of the treatment.

When grief is skipped, shame hardens into identity. When grief is honored, it becomes the soil for rebuilding.

## A Historical Note on Emotion in ADHD

When you read modern DSM criteria, you will not find emotional dysregulation as a core feature. That is a 1970s editorial choice, not a clinical truth. From 1798 — when the Scottish physician Alexander Crichton published *Diseases of Attention*, the first medical description of what we now call ADHD — through 1976, **every major theorist treated emotional impulsiveness as central to the disorder**.

> "From 1798 until 1976, emotion was part of ADHD. Every major theorist, every paper, every science paper... all included emotional impulsiveness as part of this disorder. But the DSM parsed it aside and made it an associated problem in some people. It isn't. It is as much a core feature of this disorder as is any other symptom in the DSM. And that was our mistake."
> — Russell Barkley, *30 Essential Ideas* 1B

So when you rage at small frustrations and then feel guilty about the rage — that is the disorder, not your character. The feeling is normal. Not moderating it is the deficit:

> "The feeling you're having is normal. That you are not moderating it is not. It is this inability to self-soothe, to self-calm, and to then moderate the emotion to be more acceptable for the context."
> — Barkley, *30EI 1B*

And the social cost is real and unfair:

> "Friends forgive you your distractibility, your forgetfulness, your working memory problems, and even your restlessness. They will not forgive your anger, your hostility, the quickness with which you emote to other people, because it is offensive. It is socially costly."
> — Barkley, *30EI 1B*

This is the inhibitory deficit expressed through the emotional channel. Treatment — medication, paused responses, physiological regulation — addresses it. Self-blame does not.

## What Compassion Looks Like (Not Permissiveness)

I am not telling you "be kind to yourself" as a soft-focus empty phrase. I am telling you compassion is clinically necessary because shame blocks action.

Compassion that works looks like:

- Accurate attribution (call the symptom a symptom, not a moral failing)
- Realistic calibration (use the 30% rule, not peer comparison)
- Energy honoring (you have a limited EF budget — plan for it)
- Rest without guilt (ADHD exhaustion is real — it is cognitive, not lazy)
- Treatment compliance as self-respect (taking your medication is not weakness, it is wisdom)
- Scaffolding without apology (the crutches are tools, not concessions)
- Error accommodation (you will forget things; build systems that absorb this)

Compassion that does not work looks like:

- Romanticizing ADHD as a gift to avoid treatment
- Accepting impairment as identity and not pursuing change
- Using diagnosis as an excuse to not scaffold
- Withholding accountability because "it's the ADHD"

Compassion and accountability are not opposites. You can be kind to yourself *and* take the medication, build the calendar, and ask for help.

## The First Script

For the shame episode, a simple reframe script:

1. **Name the action**: "I didn't do X."
2. **Refuse the moral frame**: "That doesn't mean I'm lazy or broken."
3. **Name the EF deficit**: "My [working memory / task initiation / time awareness] failed."
4. **Name the scaffolding gap**: "I didn't have [external reminder / sub-deadline / accountability partner] in place."
5. **Name the next step**: "I will [add reminder / ask for check-in / break task into smaller pieces]."
6. **Stop the spiral**: "This is enough self-work for now. The spiral does not help. Action does."

Read this script when the shame hits. Print it out. Keep it near your desk. Externalize it, because your brain will not summon it from memory during the storm.

## The Bottom Line on Shame

You are not lazy. You are not stupid. You are not weak. You are not a failure. You are not unlovable. You are not broken.

You have a developmental disorder of self-regulation that makes the modern world — optimized for intact executive function — disproportionately punishing to your brain. You have been absorbing the punishment for years and calling it your identity.

It is not your identity. It is the wound. Name it accurately. Treat it properly. Forgive yourself for what you did not know.

Now we work.
