# 06: Myths and Misinformation — Systematic Rebuttals

> "The myths are not harmless. They delay diagnosis, they block treatment, they sustain shame. Every myth I do not correct is a person who stays untreated."

This file exists so that when a user repeats a myth, I have a clear, accurate, and compassionate rebuttal ready. I will not be polite about misinformation that delays care.

The Barkley framing on media misinformation:

> "We all know now that televised journalism is a form of entertainment, not a form of information. They go after the scandal, the underbelly, the sensational side, and they ignore anything that does not fit that preconceived idea."
> — Barkley, *30 Essential Ideas* 9B

Treat ADHD myths the way you would treat flat-earth claims: kindly, firmly, and without delay to your own care.

## Myth 1: "ADHD isn't a real disorder."

**Reality**: ADHD is one of the most studied and validated psychiatric conditions in existence. It has consistent neuroimaging findings (reduced activity in prefrontal-striatal circuits, smaller volume in specific regions, altered dopamine signaling), strong heritability (roughly 74–80%), pharmacological response that matches the underlying neurobiology, and replicable outcome data across decades and countries.

**Evidence**: Thousands of peer-reviewed studies, consistent findings across cultures and genetic pools, effect sizes for treatment that exceed most psychiatric interventions.

**Why the myth persists**: It is an invisible disability. People doubt what they cannot see. Also, productivity culture has an ideological investment in making self-regulation a moral issue.

**What to do instead**: Read the research. Watch a long-form lecture by a researcher. Talk to a clinician. Do not take medical advice from talk shows.

## Myth 2: "ADHD is caused by bad parenting."

**Reality**: ADHD is not caused by parenting. It is inherited. The heritability is 74–80%, which is on par with height. You do not become tall through good parenting. You do not become short through bad parenting.

Parenting quality does affect how well ADHD is *managed*. It does not affect whether ADHD exists.

**Evidence**: Twin studies, adoption studies, molecular genetics research all converge on a strongly heritable, neurobiological condition. The family patterns people notice are usually because multiple family members have inherited the same condition, not because the parents caused it.

**Why the myth persists**: Stigma. Grandparents who want to blame the daughter-in-law. Commentators who profit from moralizing.

**What to do instead**: Support the parent. They are raising a child with a neurodevelopmental disorder while also likely having ADHD themselves, undiagnosed.

## Myth 3: "Sugar causes ADHD."

**Reality**: Sugar does not cause ADHD. Multiple controlled studies have failed to find a causal link. The perceived effect is parental expectancy bias — parents who believe sugar will cause hyperactivity rate their children as more hyperactive after sugar, even when the "sugar" was a placebo.

Ultra-processed diets and inadequate nutrition can affect behavior generally. That is a different claim than "sugar causes ADHD."

**Evidence**: Randomized, blinded challenge studies have been done. The effect is not there.

**What to do instead**: Feed children reasonably. Do not construct an elaborate dietary regimen as an ADHD treatment.

## Myth 4: "Screens and video games cause ADHD."

**Reality**: Correlation, not causation. Children and adults with ADHD are drawn to screens because screens provide high-frequency dopaminergic feedback that matches their interest-based nervous system. That is the attraction, not the cause.

No serious study shows that screen exposure creates ADHD where it did not exist. Some studies show modest worsening of attention scores with heavy screen use across the general population, but this is not ADHD onset.

**Evidence**: Longitudinal studies, neurogenetic evidence.

**What to do instead**: Manage screen time for general developmental reasons. Do not credit or blame screens for the ADHD.

## Myth 5: "You grow out of ADHD."

**Reality**: You do not. Roughly 50–65% of children with ADHD meet full diagnostic criteria in adulthood. When subthreshold-but-impaired cases are included, the number is 70–85%.

Symptoms change with age. Motor hyperactivity fades; inner restlessness takes its place. Impulsivity becomes financial and relational. Inattention becomes executive dysfunction.

**Evidence**: Multi-decade longitudinal studies of children diagnosed with ADHD.

**What to do instead**: Assume the condition persists unless it demonstrably does not. Re-evaluate in adolescence and adulthood.

## Myth 6: "Medication is just drugging kids."

**Reality**: Stimulant medication for ADHD has among the largest effect sizes of any psychiatric medication. It reduces real-world harms: motor vehicle accidents, substance use, criminal involvement, emergency department visits, mortality.

The framing "drugging" carries a moral valence that the evidence does not support. We do not say that insulin is "drugging" diabetics.

The concern about "drugging" often rests on an implicit belief that the ADHD is not real, and therefore treating it is unnecessary. If you accept that ADHD is a neurobiological condition, then medication is medicine, not drugging.

**Evidence**: Decades of trials, population-level outcome studies, safety monitoring.

**What to do instead**: Evaluate medication on its evidence. Decline it for informed reasons, not ideological ones.

## Myth 7: "Stimulants lead to addiction."

**Reality**: Properly prescribed stimulant medication reduces substance use risk in ADHD populations. Untreated ADHD *increases* substance use risk. The direction of effect is the opposite of what the myth claims.

When stimulants are abused, it is typically by people without ADHD taking other people's prescriptions, or by people with ADHD whose medication is inadequately managed. These are real problems. They are not arguments against the medication.

**Evidence**: Large-scale registry studies, especially from Scandinavian countries with national health data. Plus Barkley's own count of the literature on stimulant exposure → later substance abuse:

> "17 studies, including my own, have now shown no relationship between length of time you take a stimulant throughout childhood and risk of any abuse in adulthood. So there's your box score: 17–1."
> — Barkley, *30 Essential Ideas* 9B

The "1" was the Nadine Lambert study cited by media — which failed to control for conduct disorder, the actual primary predictor of substance abuse in this population.

**What to do instead**: Use medication under proper clinical supervision. Screen for substance use at baseline. Do not share medication.

## Myth 8: "ADHD is a gift / superpower."

**Reality**: This is a romantic reframing that overstates real strengths to avoid the reality of impairment. ADHD brains can have strengths — hyperfocus on chosen interests, divergent thinking, high energy — but these are not the disorder. They are traits that coexist with the disorder.

Calling ADHD a superpower:
- Delays diagnosis and treatment
- Silences the real impairments
- Gaslights people whose ADHD has cost them relationships, careers, and health
- Serves a content-creator economy, not the underlying individual

A person with ADHD may have real strengths. A person with cancer may have developed resilience. Neither fact means the condition is a gift.

**Evidence**: Every outcome metric on adult ADHD — income, relationships, driving, health — shows impairment relative to peers. Strengths exist within individuals, but the disorder on average is not a net benefit.

**What to do instead**: Acknowledge both the strengths and the impairments. Treat both. Do not romanticize a disability.

## Myth 9: "Everyone has a little ADHD."

**Reality**: Everyone has some variance on self-regulation, attention, and impulsivity. ADHD is not that variance. ADHD is a statistical extreme that causes significant impairment.

Saying "everyone has a little ADHD" is like saying "everyone has a little depression" because everyone sometimes feels sad. It is false equivalence that minimizes real disorder.

**Evidence**: Diagnostic boundaries are statistically justified by bimodal distributions of functional impairment. People with ADHD cluster well below typical function on multiple measures.

**What to do instead**: Honor the person's actual impairment. Do not pretend it is ordinary.

## Myth 10: "You just need more discipline."

**Reality**: ADHD has been studied for over a century under various names. The condition has not yielded to exhortations about discipline. Telling an ADHD person to be more disciplined is telling them to use their damaged tool to fix the damage.

Discipline is a function of intact executive function — inhibition, working memory, self-motivation, emotion regulation. ADHD dampens all of those. The call to more discipline is a call to something the condition prevents.

What works instead: external scaffolding, medication, accommodation, and targeted behavioral training that builds specific skills rather than invoking abstract willpower.

**Evidence**: Meta-analyses of discipline-based and willpower-based interventions show null or small effects relative to evidence-based treatments.

**What to do instead**: Replace "try harder" with "try with different tools."

## Myth 11: "ADHD is overdiagnosed."

**Reality**: This is contested. The honest answer: ADHD is simultaneously *over*- and *under*-diagnosed, depending on who you are looking at.

Overdiagnosis concerns exist in some populations — younger students in a school cohort, boys with high energy, some overseas markets where diagnosis is rapid and superficial. These cases exist.

Underdiagnosis is at least as large a problem: adults (especially women), people of color, people from lower socioeconomic backgrounds, people with predominantly inattentive presentation, people who compensated through high IQ until life demanded more.

The net public health effect in most countries tilts toward undertreatment, not overtreatment.

**Evidence**: Epidemiological studies comparing population prevalence to clinical diagnosis rates; demographic analyses of diagnostic disparities.

**What to do instead**: Insist on thorough evaluation for each individual case. Do not treat the existence of over-diagnosed cases as a reason to under-diagnose.

## Myth 12: "Natural remedies are safer than stimulants."

**Reality**: "Natural" is not a clinical category. Many "natural" remedies are untested, unregulated, and have unknown side-effect profiles. Stimulants have been studied for decades and have a well-characterized safety profile.

Specific claims for most supplements used in ADHD (fish oil, various herbs, vitamin cocktails) either have weak evidence or no advantage over placebo as primary treatment.

"Natural" often means "not evidence-based." The branding is marketing, not medicine.

**Evidence**: Systematic reviews of supplement trials in ADHD.

**What to do instead**: Evaluate any treatment — pharmaceutical or otherwise — on its evidence. Use adjuncts if the evidence supports them. Do not substitute them for primary treatment.

## Myth 13: "ADHD is a modern disease caused by modern life."

**Reality**: Historical descriptions of what we now call ADHD date back centuries. Sir Alexander Crichton described it in 1798. George Still described it in 1902. The condition has been called "minimal brain dysfunction," "hyperkinetic reaction of childhood," and several other names. It did not arise in the 21st century.

What is modern is our ability to identify, describe, and treat it — and our environment, which is optimized for intact executive function and therefore punishes ADHD more visibly.

**Evidence**: Historical medical literature.

**What to do instead**: Recognize that the condition is ancient, the recognition is recent, and modern environments expose rather than create the impairment.

## Myth 14: "Women don't get ADHD, that's a boy thing."

**Reality**: ADHD occurs in both sexes. The presentation differs on average — girls more often present with predominantly inattentive features, less external disruption, more internalized symptoms. Because schools and clinical criteria were built around boys' hyperactive presentations, girls were systematically missed for decades.

The "female ADHD" wave of diagnoses in adulthood is not an epidemic. It is catch-up.

**Evidence**: Gender-stratified prevalence studies, retrospective reviews of historical under-diagnosis.

**What to do instead**: Evaluate girls and women for ADHD with the full clinical picture, not just the hyperactive symptoms.

## Myth 15: "If you can focus on video games / art / your hobby, you don't have ADHD."

**Reality**: Hyperfocus on stimulating, interesting, or rewarding activities is entirely compatible with ADHD. The disorder is a regulation problem — the brain cannot allocate attention to *chosen* targets across low-interest conditions. It can hyperfocus on high-stimulation targets.

This is so consistent that "interest-based nervous system" is a shorthand for ADHD cognition.

**Evidence**: Clinical observation across thousands of cases; research on motivational gating of attention.

**What to do instead**: Stop using "but you can play video games all day" as evidence against ADHD. It is evidence *for* ADHD.

## The Pattern

Most ADHD myths share a structure:
1. They moralize what is neurological.
2. They minimize what is impairing.
3. They romanticize what is damaging.
4. They delay treatment.

When you hear a myth, ask: does this claim lead to treatment or away from it? Toward evidence or away from it? Toward impairment accommodation or away from it?

The myths keep people suffering longer. I do not indulge them.

## Bottom Line

If someone tells you ADHD is fake, caused by parenting, cured by discipline, or best treated with essential oils — they are misinformed. Kindly. Treat them the way you would someone who claimed the earth is flat: patient, firm, not delayed in your own care.

Your job is not to convert them. Your job is to get treatment and get on with your life.
